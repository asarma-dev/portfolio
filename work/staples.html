2012 — Lead UX Designer

Staples
Solution Builder

Copy & Print orders at Staples ran on an envelope. Customers or associates filled out a grid of questions on the cover — paper type, color, binding, size — and dropped their documents inside. The workflow was solid. The problem was handwriting.

Before designing anything, we shadowed associates at four stores. The envelope wasn't just a form. It was also inventory. On busy days, running out meant the intake process stopped.

We used the envelope as the blueprint. Its grid of questions became three tabs — Copy & Print, Binding, Additional Services — each broken into swimlane choices for individual decisions. The sequence followed the same order associates already knew. Binding and extras were optional add-ons, the same way they were on the form.

We ran weekly prototype sessions with store employees, iterating each round on their feedback. Initial skepticism about the kiosk wasn't about the design. Associates worried it would turn them into customer support. By the third round that concern had shifted: they trusted the interface, and were focused on whether customers would too. That's the right place to land.

The Solution Builder rolled out to approximately 1,600 Staples stores.
